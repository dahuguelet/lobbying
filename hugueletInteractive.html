Missouri's million-dollar legislature

Since 1875, Missouri legislators have taken an oath of office that includes this promise: “I will not knowingly receive, directly or indirectly, any money or other valuable thing for the performance or nonperformance of any act or duty pertaining to my office, other than the compensation allowed by law.” But the oath lacks force without an enacted limit on the amount of gifts legislators can receive from lobbyists. From October 2012 to September 2014, matching the length of time between statewide elections, legislators and their staffs and family members received $1,813,768.74 in gifts from organizations with interests in the General Assembly’s activities. In this graphic, all gifts to a legislator's staff and family members are included under the legislator's name, so amounts received should be interpreted as the total amount received by the office of each legislator.

Lobbying up 21 percent since 2010

2004 saw a record high for lobbying, when more than a milion dollars were spent. Since then Missouri hasn’t seen a million-dollar spending year. 2013's $966,000 came in second place in the past 10 years and marked a 21 percent increase from 2010's low point. *Note: 2014's total reflects gifts from January through September, as ethics data for later months have not yet been published.

No limits for Missouri lobbyists

Twenty-three states have placed limits on gifts that their state legislators receive from lobbyists. Twelve states have banned the practice. Missouri is one of the 15 states without limits on lobbyist gifts, meaning hundreds of thousands of dollars, World Series tickets and steak dinners are all fair game for anyone looking to exert influence in the legislature.

The lobbied legislators

Whether lawmakers should accept gifts from lobbyists is an issue that's been debated in newspaper columns, nightly broadcasts and the General Assembly itself. But what isn't debatable is lobbying's popularity. Legislators accepted gifts ranging from pricey 2014 World Series tickets to $2 sodas, and lobbyists didn't discriminate by party: 68 percent of gifts went to Republican legislators that control 72 percent of seats in the General Assembly, and 32 percent went to Democrats that control 28 percent of seats.

How Columbia's delegation stands

Legislators representing parts of Columbia and Boone County run the gamut in their amounts received from lobbyists, and in some cases their staff members were lobbied much more than the lawmakers themselves. Sen. Kurt Schaefer, R-Columbia, for example, is listed as personally receiving just $822.86 of the nearly $12,000 he and his staff received from lobbyists. His staff accounted for the other $10,961.99. Rep. John Wright, D-Rocheport, was one of two incumbent legislators that pledged not to accept gifts from lobbyists.

Majority of gifts go to groups

Thirty-four percent of gifts are filed in ethics reports as going to an individual legislator. The rest, nearly $1.2 million in the last 24 months, went to groups or committees within the legislature. The reports do not note whether a gift went to one legislator in a group or several recipients. The amounts range from $604,122 for the "Entire General Assembly" to $296.59 for the Standing Committee on Government Oversight and Accountability.

Select few organizations are biggest gift-givers

More than 500 firms, corporations and organizations lobbied Missouri legislators from October 2012 to September 2014. But the 13 organizations that gave more than $25,000 in gifts, which make up just 2.4 percent of all givers, represent approximately $609,000, slightly more than a third of all gifts. The top ranks are led by energy stalwart Ameren and include powerful industry organizations such as the Missouri Beverage Association and lobbying firms such as John Bardgett & Associates. Lobbyists and lawyers lead lobbying ranks

Legal and lobbying firms, featuring top-ten donors and strategic government consultants John Bardgett & Associates and Flotron & McIntosh, made up the most generous industries to legislators during the past 24 months. But the data may illustrate how the numbers could be skewed, as other organizations could hire lobbying firms to curry favor on their behalf and file an ethics report with the lobbying firm's name on it.
